Protecting Your Child's Digital Identity

How to safeguard your child's name, privacy, and digital footprint — starting from day one.

Your child has a digital footprint before they can walk. The average child appears online hundreds of times before they're old enough to consent — in birth announcements, milestone posts, and shared albums. The good news: a few simple decisions now protect their privacy and identity for years to come. Here's how.

1 Claim their name early

Names are scarce online. A .com domain and the matching handles can only belong to one person — and once a stranger registers them, they may be gone for your child's whole life. Securing your child's name early means it's theirs when they're ready for it: a homepage, a portfolio, an email address, an identity that's genuinely their own rather than a number on someone else's platform.

  • Check whether your child's name is available as a .com — and consider the variations (first + last, with a middle name, a nickname).
  • If you grab it, keep the registration details private (use domain privacy) so your contact info isn't published in public records.

2 Rethink "sharenting"

Posting your child's life publicly feels normal — but a public post is permanent, copyable, and searchable. Photos can be saved, scraped, and re-used out of context, and platforms keep their own copies under terms you didn't write. Before you post publicly, ask: would I be comfortable with this being attached to my child's name in ten years?

Why it matters: a child's photos shared publicly today can be scraped into datasets, used to train facial-recognition systems, or simply surface in a search when they're applying to schools or jobs. You can't easily un-share something once it's public.

3 Lock down your own privacy settings

Most over-sharing is accidental — a profile set to "public" by default. Spend ten minutes here:

  • Set your personal accounts to private, so only people you approve can see posts about your child.
  • Turn off location tagging on photos — geotags can reveal your home, your child's school, and your routine.
  • Review who can see past posts, and tighten "tag" and "mention" permissions.

4 Know what not to post publicly

A few things are best kept out of public view entirely:

  • Full name paired with birth date — together these are building blocks for identity theft.
  • Home address, school, or daily routine — including anything that reveals them (street signs, uniforms, location tags).
  • Bath time, potty training, medical, or embarrassing moments — your child can't consent now, and may not thank you later.
  • Anything you'd be uncomfortable a stranger saving — because, if it's public, they can.

5 Share privately instead

You don't have to choose between sharing with the people you love and protecting your child. Share in places you control:

  • A password-protected site or private album that only invited family and friends can open.
  • Close-friends lists and private group chats rather than public feeds.
  • Anything that keeps your child's story visible to your circle and invisible to search engines and scrapers.

6 Set up the basics

  • Reserve a simple email address in your child's name that you manage until they're old enough.
  • Use a password manager and turn on two-factor authentication on accounts that hold their photos and data.
  • Keep a private, organized backup of the moments that matter — somewhere that isn't a public platform.

✓ Your 10-minute checklist

  • Check if your child's name is available as a .com (and grab it before someone else does)
  • Set your personal social accounts to private
  • Turn off photo location tagging
  • Stop posting full name + birth date together publicly
  • Move your child's photos to a private, password-protected place
  • Turn on two-factor authentication where their data lives
